# What did I learn and how did I evolve in 2025?

You treated 2025 as a transition year: you shifted from “senior engineer who uses AI” to **systems** thinker intentionally re‑tooling around agentic AI, quant ML, and SaaS as your main leverage points. You also clarified that the next decade of your work will be built around modernizing SaaS and trading workflows with AI-native architectures, not just adding features.[^1]

## How your thinking evolved

- You reframed your identity from generalist full‑stack engineer to “agentic AI / ML systems architect and quant ML researcher in training,” explicitly making this your next major career arc.
- You shifted from tool-chasing to domain-mapping: instead of randomly reading AI news, you built structured roadmaps for AI-in-SaaS, multi‑agent systems, and quant research so you can learn in deliberate layers over years.[^1]
- You adopted a “systems and workflows” lens: AI is no longer a feature; it is the backbone of product design, developer workflow, and business models you care about.


## What you learned technically

- You built a detailed curriculum for agentic AI that spans Python, OOP, data-science libraries, reinforcement learning, multi‑agent systems, and evaluation, effectively creating your own grad‑level syllabus.
- You dug into 2025 literature on multi‑agent reinforcement learning and ML more broadly, tracking month‑by‑month advances and tying them to concrete use cases and startup ideas instead of reading in isolation.[^2][^3][^4]
- You explored RAG systems and coding agents (IDE agents, LangGraph/LangChain/LlamaIndex, evaluation frameworks), moving from “use ChatGPT” to “design an agentic RAG architecture and let agents write/maintain code.”[^5][^6]


## How your product and startup lens changed

- You treated “AI + SaaS modernization” as your personal brand and built a domain-knowledge roadmap to guide content, consulting, and product bets instead of chasing random SaaS ideas.[^1]
- You began viewing SaaS platforms as orchestration layers over agents and automation (workflows, copilots, evaluators, monitoring) rather than CRUD apps with a chatbot bolted on.[^7]
- You clarified that your ventures should converge on a coherent thesis: AI-native SaaS and quant platforms where your deep system-design experience plus new ML skills create moat, not just features.


## How you approached work and learning

- You leaned into long‑horizon learning: multi‑year roadmaps, capstone-style projects, and domain maps replaced “course hopping,” making your time investment compounding instead of fragmented.[^1]
- You started treating AI agents as collaborators in your workflow (PRDs, scaffolding, code generation, research synthesis) and invested in understanding how to scaffold them correctly rather than just prompting.[^5][^6]
- You accepted that the next level requires research‑grade reading and experimentation (papers, benchmarks, institutional‑grade backtesting standards), and you began moving toward that bar instead of staying in pure engineering comfort.

If you want to crystallize this further, consider writing short answers (3–5 bullets each) to:

- “What experiments in 2025 made me realize I can play at the research/architecture frontier?”[^3]
- “Which skills did I start but not yet finish (e.g., MARL implementations, full RAG platform, backtesting infra), and what’s the explicit 2026 plan to close those loops?”
- “Which parts of SaaS modernization and quant ML felt most energizing vs. draining, and what does that say about the work I should double down on?”
